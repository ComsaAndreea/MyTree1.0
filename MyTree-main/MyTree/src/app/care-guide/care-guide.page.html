Care Guide
The Pine Tree:   1. Inspect your young pine tree for wounds on its trunk and main branches. Use care when transporting it home so not to break branches.

  2. Search for a large space to plant the pine tree, as these trees belong to the bigger category. (height: 50'-80', width: 20'-40')

  3. As Fall comes, it will drop a lot of needles; make sure not to plant where you don't want those!

  4. Be sure to properly plant trees. Installing them incorrectly or leaving stakes and wires on trees often causes tree failure before
      they even reach maturity.

  5. "Shaping" the tree is not advised as its soft wood will be vulnerable to snow, ice or wind damage.

  6. Don’t mow underneath your pine trees. When you install a young tree, extend a mulch bed around its base that gives at least 12″-18″
      of mulched areas between the tree and turfgrass. As the tree grows, expand the mulch bed out further, always
      keeping ahead of the growth.

  7. Pines will shed old needles each fall.
      Allow these needles to fall under your trees; it will help to improve the soil with compost for your trees.



The Birch Tree:   1. The birch tree is very sensitive to heat and drought. It needs moist, cool soil, but also sunshine on its leaves to flourish.

  2. When planting a birch tree select a site that will shade its roots in the afternoon and still provide sun to canopy for much of the day.

  3. Mulching is recommended for this tree as well.

  4. Birch trees are susceptible to borers as well as other insects and tree diseases.
      A preventive insect control program is recommended to reduce problems.

  5. Keep your birch trees as healthy as possible through regular monitoring, pruning, watering and using birch tree fertilizer.

  6. Broken, diseased, or dead branches should be removed in order to prevent decay-producing fungi from infecting other areas of the birch tree.

  7. Removing branch stubs promotes successful and proper healing of wounds.



The Apple Tree:   1. There are many diseases and fruit pests that attack apples so it’s rare for this fruit to be grown without any type of pesticides.
      It will take much research and persistance to grow apples is you wish to avoid any type of spraying program.

  2. The apple tree will be needing "friends"; most apples need pollen from another apple tree to produce fruit. This second tree must be
      a different cultivar but also one that will flower at the same time.

  3. The presence of bees will be very important; poor pollination can reduce the number of fruit and cause misshapen fruit.

  4. Apples produce best when grown in full sun.

  5. Apple trees need well-drained soil, but should be able to retain some moisture.

  6. Plant fruit in a location with good air circulation so leaves dry quickly after a rainfall or irrigation.

  7. The ideal soil pH is 6.0 to 6.5 but a pH range of 5.5 to 7.0 is acceptable.




The Cherry Tree:  1. Plant cherry trees in early spring or late fall!

  2. The area should be a sunny site with good air circulation and deep, well-drained soil.

  3. After flowering in a fruiting year, you’ll need to drape trees with wildlife-safe netting to protect the fruit from birds.

  4. Mulching is recommended.

  5. For sweet cherries, make sure the different varieties will pollinate each other.

  6. For bareroot trees, place the rootstock on a small mound of soil in the center of the planting hole, and spread the roots down and away, trying not
      to bend the roots. Backfill with soil.
      For container-grown trees, first remove the rootball and on its side; cut through the roots with shears. Don’t cover the top of the root-ball.

  7. Fertilize each spring until trees start to bear, then fertilize only after harvest each season.
